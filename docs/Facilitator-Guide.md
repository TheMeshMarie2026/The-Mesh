📘 The Mesh — Classroom Facilitation Guide for Instructors
1. Purpose of The Mesh
The Mesh is a crisis‑simulation game that teaches:

systems thinking

cross‑disciplinary collaboration

trust dynamics

resource management

negotiation and governance

resilience under uncertainty

It works beautifully in classrooms, workshops, leadership programs, and corporate trainings.

Your role as facilitator is to guide the experience, not control it.

2. What You Need Before Class
Materials
Projector or shared screen

Student devices (laptops/tablets)

Team roster (who belongs to which node)

Crisis engine ready to generate turns

Rulebook + quickstart guide (printed or digital)

Room Setup
Teams seated together

Enough space for discussion

Whiteboard or shared notes for tracking insights

Timing
A typical session runs:

Short demo: 20–30 minutes

Full simulation: 60–90 minutes

Deep-dive workshop: 2–3 hours

3. How to Introduce the Game
Your opening script can be simple:

“You’re about to enter a crisis simulation where every decision reshapes a living network.
Your team represents a node — a corporate unit, a student group, a research lab, a community partner.
Your goal is to stabilize the system through collaboration.”

Then show the onboarding wizard or quickstart guide.

Keep the tone energetic and curious.

4. Team Roles
Each team should assign:

📣 Spokesperson
Presents the team’s decision each turn.

🧠 Analyst
Reads the crisis narrative and identifies key risks.

🤝 Diplomat
Coordinates with other teams.

📊 Recorder
Tracks trust, resources, and influence changes.

🎯 Strategist
Keeps the team focused on long-term stability.

Teams can rotate roles each turn.

5. Running a Turn (Facilitator Workflow)
Step 1 — Display the Crisis Narrative
Read it aloud or let teams read silently.

Step 2 — Give Teams Time to Discuss
Recommended: 3–5 minutes  
Encourage them to:

identify stakeholders

consider trust/resource implications

think about cascading effects

talk to other teams

Step 3 — Collect Decisions
Each team chooses one of the three actions.

Step 4 — Apply the Action
The crisis engine updates the network.

Step 5 — Debrief Briefly
Ask:

“What did you expect to happen?”

“What surprised you?”

“Who gained trust? Who lost it?”

Keep it short — the game’s momentum matters.

6. Facilitator Tips for Smooth Play
✔ Encourage cross-team communication
The Mesh thrives when teams talk across disciplines.

✔ Don’t explain the backend
Players don’t need to know the graph mechanics.

✔ Keep the pace brisk
Momentum keeps engagement high.

✔ Celebrate smart collaboration
Highlight moments where teams solve problems together.

✔ Let failure happen
Collapse is a valid outcome — and a powerful learning moment.

✔ Use reflection questions
After each turn:

“What changed in the network?”

“Who became more influential?”

“What trust dynamics emerged?”

7. Handling Common Classroom Situations
Students freeze or overthink
Give them a nudge:

“Pick the action that feels most aligned with your team’s strengths.”

Teams argue
Remind them:

“Conflict is part of the simulation — use negotiation actions.”

One team dominates
Introduce:

“Trust and influence shift every turn. No team stays on top forever.”

Teams ignore each other
Prompt:

“You’re not alone in this crisis. Who should you talk to?”

The crisis escalates quickly
Frame it positively:

“This is what real systems do under stress. Let’s see how you adapt.”

8. Debriefing the Entire Simulation
A strong debrief transforms gameplay into learning.

Ask these questions:
What patterns emerged in the network?

Which actions had the biggest impact?

How did trust shape your decisions?

What surprised you about collaboration?

Where did the system stabilize?

Where did it break?

What would you do differently next time?

Connect to real-world contexts:
organizational behavior

crisis leadership

cross-functional teamwork

supply chain resilience

campus governance

technical incident response

9. Optional Extensions
📚 Homework or reflection
Write a short analysis of your team’s strategy.

Map the trust dynamics across turns.

Propose a new action type for the game.

🏛 Leadership workshop
Have teams present:

their crisis strategy

their negotiation approach

their coalition-building tactics

🎓 Classroom assessment
Evaluate:

collaboration

communication

systems thinking

adaptability

10. Facilitator Closing Script
“The Mesh shows how systems behave under stress — and how people behave inside systems.
You didn’t just play a game. You practiced crisis leadership, collaboration, and resilience.”
